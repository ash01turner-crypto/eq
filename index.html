◎Interactive Scenario

Project Polaris

You’re leading a high-stakes project. Your choices shape delivery, trust and burnout.

You’re the project lead for Project Polaris – a critical upgrade that must go live before the end of the quarter.

Your team is a mix of veterans and newer staff, and senior leaders have made it very clear: this one can’t slip.

It’s Monday morning. You’ve just grabbed your coffee and opened your inbox. Three things jump out at you:

  • A message from your sponsor asking for a “quick status update” by lunchtime.
  • A calendar clash between your project stand-up and a key stakeholder workshop.
  • A note from HR flagging that one of your team members, Wes, has had several recent absences.

Where do you focus first?

Team Stand-up

You gather the team in a small meeting room. People look tired. A couple of them check their phones as they sit down.

“How’s everyone going?” you ask.

Silence. Then Tash, your senior analyst, speaks up.

“We’re making progress, but the requirements keep changing. And the testing window is shrinking. If we keep adding scope, we’re going to miss the date.”

Some team members nod. Others look away.

How do you respond?

Sponsor Call

You jump on a quick call with your sponsor, Karen.

“Just checking in,” she says. “The executives want reassurance we’re still on track. I’d like to tell them we’re green.”

You know there are risks: shrinking testing time, some unclear requirements, and a tired team. On paper, the date is still just about achievable.

What do you say?

HR Concern: Wes

You open the HR note about Wes. Several recent sick days cluster around long weekends and key project activities.

HR has asked you to “check in and provide support” and to note any impact on delivery.

You find Wes in a quiet corner after the stand-up.

He looks tired and a little defensive. “Is this about my sick leave?” he asks.

How do you start the conversation?

Creating Psychological Safety

You take a breath.

“I appreciate the honesty,” you say. “If we’re going to hit this date without burning everyone out, I need to know about risks early. Let’s list what’s worrying us most, then we’ll decide together what to escalate or re-plan.”

You grab a whiteboard and invite input. Slowly, people start contributing. Tash outlines the impact of scope changes. Sam flags a dependency with another team that’s slipping.

By the end of the meeting you have a clearer picture and a short list of actions.

What’s your next move?

Pushing the Deadline

You smile tightly.

“I hear you,” you say, “but we committed to this date. Let’s stay positive and just push through. We’ll fine-tune the details as we go.”

The room goes quiet. A couple of people exchange glances. No one raises any further risks.

On the surface, things seem under control. Underneath, people start making quiet compromises and assumptions.

Where do you go next?

Digging into Scope

You lean forward.

“Okay, let’s get specific. What exactly changed, and who requested it?”

The next 30 minutes are spent mapping recent change requests. You discover that several “small tweaks” from different stakeholders add up to a lot of extra work.

You realise you need to decide how to handle this.

Honest Status

You decide to be transparent.

“Karen, we can still aim for the target date,” you say, “but we’re carrying some real risks: expanding scope, limited testing time, and a stretched team. I recommend we report amber with a clear plan: either we lock scope now, add resources, or accept that quality could be impacted.”

There’s a pause.

“I appreciate the honesty,” she says. “Send me a short summary of those options. I’ll socialise it with the execs. In the meantime, do what you can to stabilise scope.”

You’ve traded short-term comfort for long-term credibility.

What do you do next?

Over-Promising

You take the easy path.

“We’re green,” you say. “There are always a few bumps, but the team is committed. We’ll do what it takes.”

Karen sounds relieved.

“Great. I’ll tell the execs we’re on track. Keep up the good work.”

You end the call with a knot in your stomach. The risks are still there. Now they’re invisible to everyone except you and the team.

What next?

Asking for Support

You steer the conversation.

“Before we talk status colours, we need to stabilise scope,” you say. “We’re getting changes from multiple directions. If we can agree what’s truly in for this release, we can protect the date and the team.”

Karen sighs.

“Fair point. Draft a simple ‘in / out’ list and I’ll back you up with the other leaders.”

You’ve just bought yourself some political support.

Supportive Conversation with Wes

You sit down so you’re at the same level as Wes.

“This is about making sure you’re okay,” you say. “I’ve noticed a few recent absences and some missed meetings. I’m not here to accuse you of anything, I just want to understand what’s going on and how we can support you.”

Wes exhales slowly.

“It’s been rough at home. I’ve been trying to juggle things, but I know I’ve let the team down.”

Together, you explore options: flexible hours, clearer expectations, and which meetings are truly critical.

Performance First

You cross your arms.

“Wes, your absences are impacting Polaris. We need you present and reliable. This can’t continue.”

He stiffens.

“I was sick,” he says. “I’m doing my best.”

The conversation becomes defensive. You leave with a commitment to “improve”, but no real understanding of what’s happening.

Putting Wes on Hold

You skim the email, sigh, and mark it as unread to “deal with later”.

The week rolls on. Wes keeps missing some key sessions. The team quietly works around him, but resentment builds.

This will come back later.

Managing Scope

You set up a quick meeting with the key stakeholders.

“Here’s the reality,” you say. “Every ‘small’ change adds up. If we keep adding, we either move the date or compromise quality. For this release, we need a clear line.”

After some back-and-forth, you get agreement on a frozen scope for Polaris. A few nice-to-haves are pushed to a later phase.

The team breathes a little easier.

Calling for Overtime

You gather the team.

“I know it’s a big ask,” you say, “but we need a final push. If we put in some extra hours this week, we can keep the date and then ease off.”

Some people reluctantly nod. Others stare at the floor.

Over the next week, delivery accelerates. So does fatigue.

Scope Workshop

In a short virtual workshop, you walk stakeholders through a simple board: Now / Next / Later.

By the end, everyone can see that trying to do everything “now” is impossible. Some features move into “Next” and “Later”.

You’ve turned a messy requirements stream into a prioritised backlog.

Closing the Loop with the Team

You call the team together.

“I shared our risks and ideas with Karen,” you say. “She’s backing us to lock scope and protect testing. The workshop you helped shape made a real difference.”

There are a few surprised smiles. People sit a little straighter.

Tash grins. “So speaking up actually helped?”

You’ve just reinforced that raising risks is welcome.

Healthy Pace

You consciously set a new tone.

You make space in stand-ups to talk about workload and capacity. You encourage people to flag when they’re stretched. You coordinate with other leaders to avoid unnecessary meetings.

You still work hard, but in a more focused way. Defects are caught early. People stay engaged.

Hard Driver

You double down on delivery.

Overtime becomes the norm. People stop raising concerns because they don’t feel heard. Shortcuts creep into the work: test steps skipped, documentation left “for later”.

You might still make the date, but at a cost.

Silent Fixer

You try to solve everything quietly: chasing stakeholders one-on-one, patching scope and schedule issues yourself.

The team sees the stress but doesn’t quite know what’s happening. They don’t see how their input connects to decisions.

The system keeps moving, but people feel like cogs, not contributors.

Plan with Wes

You agree with Wes on:

  • Which meetings he must attend.
  • How he’ll signal if he’s struggling.
  • When you’ll check in again.

You also quietly redistribute some non-critical tasks to ease his load.

Over the next few weeks, his attendance improves and the team notices he’s more present.

Vague Support

You tell Wes, “Just do your best, I know it’s busy,” and leave it at that.

Without clear expectations or support, the pattern continues: occasional absences, missed handovers, quiet frustration from others.

The issue never properly resolves.

Monitoring Wes

You start tracking Wes’s attendance and meeting behaviour closely. Every slip is noted.

He senses the scrutiny and withdraws even further. The team begins to treat him as a weak link, not a colleague.

Sideling Wes

You move critical tasks away from Wes without fully explaining why.

He notices, and so does the rest of the team. It solves some short-term reliability problems, but it also sends a message: people are replaceable, not supported.

Go-Live Day

Weeks pass. Decision by decision, you’ve shaped how Polaris unfolded.

On the morning of go-live, the atmosphere around the team reflects the path you chose.

How Did Project Polaris End?

In a training setting, you can debrief here: which outcome feels most familiar, and what would you do differently?

Outcome: Sustainable Success

Polaris goes live close to the original date. There are a few minor issues, but nothing critical. The sponsor is satisfied, and more importantly, the team feels proud of what they achieved.

People talk about Polaris as a project where:

  • Risks were surfaced early,
  • Leaders were honest and fair,
  • Workload was tough but manageable.

You delivered the project and strengthened the team.

Outcome: Burnt-Out Delivery

Polaris technically goes live on time, but it’s messy.

There are late-night fixes, urgent patches, and frayed tempers. A couple of people quietly start looking for other roles. The sponsor congratulates you on “getting it over the line,” but you know the cost.

People talk about Polaris as:

  • “That death-march project”,
  • A place where speaking up didn’t matter,
  • A win for the schedule, not for the people.

You delivered the project, but burned a lot of goodwill.

Outcome: Slipped Project

Polaris misses its original date. Scope crept steadily, risks went unaddressed, and people were never quite on the same page.

The sponsor is frustrated. The team is deflated. Trust in the project decreases, and future work will be under a much harsher spotlight.

People talk about Polaris as:

  • “The one that got away”,
  • A lesson in what happens when issues are left to fester,
  • A reminder that inaction is also a decision.

You protected no one and still didn’t deliver.
